# Complexity Science and Generative Art

Complexity science is the branch of science devoted to studying systems across all scientific disciplines. [[Philip Galanter]] argues that complexity science provides the theoretical framework for understanding and unifying all forms of generative art — from historical to contemporary, digital to non-digital.

## Why Complexity Science?

### Transcending Technology-Centric Definitions

Rather than defining generative art through specific technologies (computers, algorithms, etc.), complexity science offers a systems-based lens that applies equally to:
- The [[Jacquard loom]] (mechanical systems)
- Algorithmic music composition (digital systems)
- Chemical reactions and crystallization (physical systems)
- Biological and ecological systems

This allows for a unified theory of generative art that is not contingent on any particular technological era.

### Systems Taxonomy

Complexity science provides frameworks for:
- Classifying different types of generative systems
- Comparing systems across domains and eras
- Understanding emergence and self-organization
- Examining how simple rules produce complex outputs

## Shared Principles Across Systems

The study of systems in complexity science reveals common principles found in all generative art:
- Autonomy (relative to human control)
- Emergence (complex outputs from simple rules)
- Feedback loops and recursion
- Non-determinism or apparent randomness
- Self-organization

## Historical and Theoretical Implications

By grounding generative art in complexity science rather than computer science, the field gains:
- Legitimacy across multiple academic disciplines
- Historical depth (connecting contemporary digital art to pre-digital practices)
- Predictive power for identifying new forms of generative art yet to be invented
