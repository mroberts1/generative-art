# Generative Art Definition

Generative art refers to any art practice in which the artist uses a system, such as a set of natural language rules, a computer program, a machine, or other procedural invention, that is set into motion with some degree of autonomy, thereby contributing to or resulting in a completed work of art.

This definition was offered by [[Philip Galanter]] in 2003 and has become the most widely cited formulation in the field.

## Key Principles

### Ceding Control to an External System

The defining characteristic of generative art is that the artist uses an external system and relinquishes partial or total control to it. This moves the discussion of generative art away from tool-based definitions toward a theory focused on systems, their role, their relationship to creativity and authorship, and system taxonomies.

### Weak Autonomy

Generative art requires only a *weak form of autonomy* from its systems. Drawing terminology from robotics, an autonomous system is one that does not require moment-to-moment decision-making or control by the artist. Systems are functionally autonomous *relative to the artist*, not in the philosophical sense of requiring free will or consciousness.

### Not Synonymous with Computer Art

A critical clarification: generative art is not a subset of [[computer art]] or digital art. While contemporary discussions often conflate these terms, generative systems can include:
- Chemical reactions
- Living organisms
- Crystallization processes
- Melting substances
- Self-organization and self-assembly
- Physical processes yet to be discovered

### Procedural Invention

The term "procedural invention" encompasses far more than computers. Historical examples like the [[Jacquard loom]] (1805) demonstrate that generative art predates computers by nearly two centuries.

## Distinguishing Generative from Rule-Based Art

Not all rule-based art is generative. Artists like Josef Albers, Piero Manzoni, and Ed Ruscha created rule-based works, but they retained moment-to-moment control over the execution. Their rules were suggestive rather than fully deterministic, and the artist never ceded functional autonomy to an external system.

For art to be generative, the system must have the authority to determine outcomes without ongoing artist intervention.

## Historical Scope

The definition is inclusive enough to encompass:
- Past generative art (e.g., mathematical systems by minimalists like [[Sol LeWitt]])
- Current practices across [[music]], [[visual art]], [[architecture]], and [[design]]
- Post-digital and non-digital generative work yet to be invented
