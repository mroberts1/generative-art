# L-Systems

L-systems (Lindenmayer systems) are a formal grammar and algorithmic approach to modeling the growth and structure of plants and other organic forms. They represent a key technique in procedural generation and are widely used in computer graphics and generative art.

## How L-Systems Work

L-systems use mathematical rules and recursion to generate complex organic structures from simple initial conditions. By iteratively applying rewriting rules, the system produces increasingly detailed plant-like morphologies without requiring manual choreography of every structural detail.

## Application in Computer Graphics

In the context of computer graphics and animation, L-systems are used to:
- "Grow" digital plants with botanical accuracy
- Generate terrain and natural structures
- Create fractal-like patterns with organic characteristics
- Reduce manual labor in animation and visual effects

Rather than hand-choreographing every branch, leaf, and stem position, artists define the generative rules and allow the system to produce complex results.

## Historical Development

The work by Prusinkiewicz, Lindenmayer, and Hanan on L-systems for plant growth has been documented extensively, particularly in publications from [[SIGGRAPH]] and related graphics research venues.

## Application in Modern Generative Art

L-systems remain a foundational technique in generative design, appearing in:
- Procedural architecture and parametric design
- Generative music composition (through structural analogs)
- Interactive and installation-based art
