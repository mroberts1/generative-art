# Autonomy in Generative Systems

Autonomy in generative art refers to the functional independence of a system from moment-to-moment control by the artist. This is a weak, pragmatic notion of autonomy distinct from philosophical conceptions requiring free will or consciousness.

## Weak vs. Strong Autonomy

Generative art systems are "autonomous" only in the weak sense borrowed from robotics. They do not require:
- Free will or consciousness
- Philosophical independence
- Freedom from human oversight

Rather, they require only **functional autonomy relative to the artist** — the system operates without needing moment-to-moment decision-making or intervention from the creator.

## Comparison with Robotics

The terminology is borrowed directly from robotics, where systems are classified by their degree of autonomy:

- **Controlled robots:** Operated moment-by-moment by a human at a console (like radio-controlled model cars or airplanes)
- **Autonomous robots:** Use sensors, GPS, image processing, and other technologies to navigate and adapt to their environment without human drivers

Generative art systems are "autonomous" in the second sense without any claims regarding consciousness or free will.

## Criticism and Clarification

Some critics object to the use of "autonomy" in generative art on two grounds:

1. **Dependency objection:** No mechanical system can be completely autonomous because all systems depend on humans for operation and maintenance.

2. **Philosophical objection:** Autonomy in the philosophical sense requires free will and consciousness, which this definition does not (and should not) claim.

Galanter's framework sidesteps these objections by adopting the functional definition used in robotics: autonomy is simply the absence of real-time human steering.

## Key Distinction from Control

The ceding of control is the essential feature of generative art. This distinguishes it from:
- Interactive art where the artist retains moment-to-moment decision-making
- Tool-based art where the artist directs every detail
- Rule-based art where rules are suggestive rather than deterministic
