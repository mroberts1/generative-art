## Summary

Philip Galanter's foundational 2016 chapter defines generative art as art created by systems set into motion with relative autonomy from moment-to-moment artist control. The work is essential for understanding generative art as a systems-based practice rooted in complexity science, not just a computer-era phenomenon. Galanter argues generative art encompasses historical practices (from Jacquard looms to Sol LeWitt rule-based systems) and extends beyond digital media.

## Key Points

- Generative art is defined as art created by autonomous or semi-autonomous systems, not direct artist manipulation
- The field has grown exponentially since 2000, often conflated with "computer art," but predates computers
- Generative art can use any system: mechanical (Jacquard loom), conceptual (Sol LeWitt instructions), biological, algorithmic
- Complexity science provides a unified theoretical framework spanning all systems-based art
- Core question: distinction between "art" (low bar) and "good art" (high, contentious bar)
- Generative art communities span computer music (1950s onward), visual arts, design, interactive systems
- Key concept: autonomy doesn't mean independence; the artist designs the system, then lets it generate
